1. Finishing Performance Analysis

** Overall Distribution
Across the 90 fighters in this dataset, the average finishing rate comes out to 52.11% (SD: 14.96). Since UFC fights end one of three ways: TKO, submission, or decision. This means roughly half of all wins in the sample come by finish rather than going the distance.

** Spread of the Data
A standard deviation of nearly 15 points signals real diversity in fighter profiles. Finishing rates range all the way from 0% to 85.7%, which roughly maps onto two ends of a spectrum: pure finishers on one side, decisiongrinders on the other.
The median (50.8%) lands close to the mean, so the distribution doesn't lean heavily in either direction which it's fairly symmetric.

** Defining "Elite Finishers"
The 75th percentile of the finishing rate distribution sits at 63.6%. Fighters at or above that mark the top quarter of the sample, 24 fighters in total are classified here as elite finishers. 


2. Rare Elite Tier (Top 10%)

**Defining the Tier
The 90th percentile threshold for finishing rate comes out to 72.7%. The 9 fighters who clear this bar make up what I'm calling the "rare elite" tier, the very top of the finishing performance curve.

** Age and Statistical Significance
The average age in this rare elite group is 28.6, compared to 32.7 across the full sample a gap of roughly 4 years.
An independent samples t-test confirms this isn't just noise: p = 0.004, well below the 0.05 threshold. The age difference between the rare elite tier and the overall sample is statistically significant.

** Physical Prime and Performance
The younger average age in this tier lines up with what's typically considered a fighter's physical prime (roughly 26–32). It suggests finishing ability isn't purely a function of technical experience. Explosiveness, speed, and conditioning likely play a real role too.


3. Finishing Method: TKO vs. Submission

** The Numbers
Within the rare elite tier, the average fighter has 9.0 TKO wins and 4.3 submission wins roughly a 2.1:1 TKO-to-submission ratio.
Fighting Style Breakdown

Among these 9 fighters, primary styles split as follows:

-Striking: 5 fighters (56%)
-Wrestling: 1 fighter
-Grappling variants (BJJ, Judo, Freestyle): 3 fighters

** Why Striking Dominates?
The majority striking composition of this tier (56%) helps explain the skew in the TKO/SUB ratio. At the elite level, striking based finishes knockouts, doctor stoppages appear to occur more often than submission finishes.

A few possible explanations:
-Striking offense tends to create finishing opportunities earlier in a fight (first round TKOs).
-Setting up a submission generally requires more time and sustained positional control.
-Takedown defense has improved across modern MMA, which cuts down on ground game opportunities.
-Striking specialists may simply be overrepresented in this particular tier (a possible selection bias).

NOTE!!!! : These findings are correlational, not causal drawing firmer conclusions would require a more controlled analysis.


4. Country Level Findings and Style Diversity

** Russian Overrepresentation
Three of the 9 rare elite fighters are Russian (33.3%). Russians make up only 11.1% of the overall sample (10 of 90) meaning Russian fighters are roughly 3x overrepresented in the rare elite tier relative to their share of the dataset.
This lines up with what you'd expect given Russia's (and Dagestan's in particular) systematic combat sports infrastructure and deep wrestling/sambo tradition.
Style Diversity Among Russian Fighters

The three Russian fighters in this tier show notably different profiles:

- Khamzat Chimaev - 6 TKO / 6 SUB (perfectly balanced)
- Usman Nurmagomedov — 8 TKO / 6 SUB (wrestling-heavy)
- Shara Magomedov — 12 TKO / 0 SUB (pure striker)

Shara Magomedov's profile stands out in particular: despite his Dagestani background, he has zero submission wins. His 12 TKO finishes mark him as a striking first fighter, which runs against the wrestling heavy stereotype often associated with that region.
A Sign of MMA's Evolution

This diversity points to something broader: modern MMA increasingly rewards a well rounded skill set over specialization in a single discipline. Seeing striking focused fighters emerge even from regions with deep wrestling roots reflects how much the sport and the strategies fighters use to adapt has evolved.
Khamzat Chimaev's even 6-6 TKO/SUB split is a textbook "complete fighter" profile, while Shara Magomedov's pure-striking approach represents the opposite end: deliberate stylistic specialization.


5. The "Complete Fighter" Profile: Khamzat Chimaev

** Peak Performance
Within the rare elite tier, Khamzat Chimaev holds the highest finishing rate at 85.7%. 12 of his 14 career wins have come by finish.

** A Genuinely Balanced Finishing Profile
Chimaev is the only fighter in this tier with equal proficiency in both finishing methods:

-6 TKO wins (striking / ground-and-pound)
-6 submission wins (grappling)
-TKO/SUB ratio: 1.0 (perfect balance)

That balance stands out sharply against the tier's average TKO/SUB ratio of 2.1:1.

** The Unpredictability Factor
Being equally dangerous on the feet and on the ground gives a fighter a real tactical edge:

-A striking threat forces opponents to prepare for the ground game.
-A grappling threat forces opponents to shore up their striking defense.
-Facing both threats at once makes building a clear game plan significantly harder.

This versatility is arguably one of the core reasons behind Chimaev's elite level performance.
